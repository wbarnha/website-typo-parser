NOTES

 


January 20, 2019

Why Digital?

ODEweb is an interactive digital textbook for the first course in ordinary differential equations (ODEs) for undergraduates in science, engineering, economics, and mathematics and consists of 27 lectures, Appendices, plus corresponding exercises. ODEweb requires an Internet connection. I developed ODEweb to add a dynamic component to the static material in printed books. This is accomplished in two ways:

(1) Nonlinear Layout: hyperlinks and notelinks: Hyperlinks (identified by blue/green type) take the reader back (or forward) to referenced material thereby saving the reader from scrolling in search of examples, definitions, theorems, etc. Notelinks (identified by magenta type) contain material which can be skipped on first reading so as not to interrupt the reader’s flow. Typical notelinks contain proofs, calculations, and historical references. A click on a notelink pops open material which in a print book would be a footnote or endnote. The web pages are coded in MathML and are rendered for all major browsers by MathJax, now the gold standard for mathematics on the web. Some special advantages of the MathJax rendering are its support for accessibility (i.e., scale mathematics type), copy and paste mathematics to MS Word, Mathematica, LaTeX documents, and integration to other web platforms (e.g., WordPress, Drupal, and Wikis).

(2) Interacts: The dynamic feature, which I call interacts, are based on Mathematica notebooks and make this presentation unique. With textual material and interacts for readers to explore solutions and their properties, as well as to investigate the results of changing parameters, both symbolically and graphically, this approach to online content promises to eclipse current offerings in this area. There is no need to load external applications - all interactivity is set within the web page. For instance, a manipulative plot remains embedded in whatever text surrounds it. Motivation, explanation and examples of concepts get first priority; detailed computations and proofs are more secondary. The interacts are built upon the Wolfram Cloud wherein all computations are done.
 

Content – Goals

This new lecture format arose from the need to provide ample motivation, insight, and understanding in my introductory ordinary differential equations (ODEs) course. Far too often students get lost in the details of determining a solution formula to an ODE. Because the actual use of ODEs goes beyond such calculations, students cannot rely on the "plug ‘n’ chug" methods that adequately served most of them in calculus. Consequently, I based my lectures on four primary goals:

1.  To develop techniques for obtaining solutions for first and second order types of ODEs;

2. To squeeze as much information as possible about its solution from the ODE without solving the ODE, even when a closed-form solution is possible;

3. To employ a variety of graphical tools to lend insight to the behavior of solutions; again even when a closed-form solution is possible; and

4. To motivate solutions methods and analysis tools by recourse to examples from the "real-world"


Content – Approach

I employ a variety of approaches to enhance the reader's understanding of ODEs. Interacts demonstrate all sort of dynamically controlled “what if’s”, variable slope and direction fields, and parameter dependence of solutions to ODEs. Visualization tools provide a 3D framework for interpretation of implicit solutions to first-order ODEs. Such tools also provide a 3D representation of solutions to 2nd order (or 2D-systems) and their phase plane projections. Applications are interspersed and integrated throughout the Lectures. Examples are drawn from population models, mechanics, biochemical-kinetics, electric circuits, ocean circulation-salinity interactions, and many other areas. The applications not only add a sense of relevance to the study of ODEs, but also provide a common point of experience by which we can analyze and interpret solutions to ODEs. I make a great effort to motivate most topics and to provide interpretations of a geometric or physical nature.

The style of the lectures is based in part on successful teaching strategies that I have used over many years. Typically, I  introduce a new topic with a motivating example that demonstrates its need. In other words, I attempt to answer the question, "What is this good for?" Sometimes I provide a "working definition" while properties of the topic are being explored. Another example on the topic is detailed with steps that suggest a generalization. When appropriate, I outline a solution procedure that is followed by yet another example. At this point, if relevant, I introduce formal material. After a concluding example, I provide an explanation, a justification, or even a proof. The nonlinear and interactive features of a web-based treatment of ODEs offer learning opportunities for students heretofore unavailable. I expect that this kind of format will dominate scientific publishing within a few years.

Content – Special Features

  • Graphical and physical for a better understanding of solution behavior models.
  • Brief solution procedures for some common ODE forms.
  • Routine calculations in many examples, normally “left to the reader,” are found in Notelinks.
  • Alerts: Specific areas where students are more inclined to make a mistake are set off by an "ALERT."
  • Built-in graphing software to instantly plot explicit or implicitly defined solutions to ODEs.
  • Applications demonstrate the need for theory and illuminate the theory. Many of the applications are new for a book at this level; e.g., ; ocean circulation-salinity interaction, biochemical-kinetics, a seismometer, and collapse of the Tacoma Narrows Bridge based on aeroelastic flutter.
  • A review of linear algebra (Appendix A) and complex numbers (Apendix B)
  • A complete hyperlinked index that complements the lectures.


Coverage

I have chosen to emphasize qualitative properties of ODEs and their solutions. This complements the availability of easy to use software, such as Wolfram Alpha, for instant calculations. The 27 lectures cover most, but not all, of the traditional topics in an introductory ODE course for students in STEM programs.  Series solutions to singular linear 2nd –order ODEs, higher order linear ODEs, boundary value problems, and more extensive development of numerical methods are excluded since topics are rarely covered in the one semester introductory course in ODEs. [The basic Euler method for 1st –order and systems of 1st –order systems are covered.] Some special topics included here which are not found in many other books are

  • Emphasis on piecewise inputs.
  • Extensive development of existence, uniqueness, extendability, and continuous dependence.
  • Operator methods and Green's kernel to solve nonhomogeneous second-order ODEs.
  • Graphical interpretation of convolution.
  • Matrix exponential solution to a linear system.

 

Exercises

An extensive set of exercises is provided for each lecture. Some exercises are based on use of the Interacts. The exercises are grouped into Basic and Advanced categories. The latter require more analysis and thought. The use of Mathematica software to solve ODEs as well as to plot and analyze solutions is encouraged. Wolfram Alpha can be used by those readers who lack access to Mathematica. Where appropriate, I have included hyperlinks from some exercises to relevant lecture material.

Implementation

I used an initial version of these lectures and exercises during the 2005-2008 academic years and again in 2014-2018 for Math 214 and Math 216 (Honors ODE). Each class of 75 minutes meets twice a week for 14 weeks. Of the 28 meetings, I devote three to exams. This leaves 25 meetings to cover the course material. I cover almost all of the material with the exception of Lectures 9, 13, and 27. In 2014-2015, three other GMU faculty taught from ODEweb. Their feedback has been extremely valuable. I also used my material in the honors version of this course in spring 2014, 2017, and 2018 where I could place more emphasis on topics such as bifurcation and nonlinear systems.

ODEweb is designed to be used as a required text for a course in ODEs or as a supplemental source for such a course. ODEweb may also be used for professional development purposes. Solutions to the exercises may be posted at the course website. I have done this for all of the assigned exercises in Math 214 and have posted a substantial number of solutions for non-assigned exercises as well.

Prerequisites and Audience Level

It is assumed that the reader has had the standard three-semester calculus sequence. Some familiarity with matrices and complex numbers would be nice, although supplements offer brief summaries of this basic material. While I teach the course to a broad range of students, ODEweb is aimed at students with upper-middle ability, However, enough detail is included in many of the procedures, examples, and applications so as to reach out to some of the less-prepared students. To those students with better than average preparation and ability, ODEweb includes many advanced features, such as continuous dependence, a geometric interpretation of convolution, and a wide array of interesting examples which are not normally included at this level.

Technical Matters

The computations that power the interacts take place in the Wolfram Cloud. Consequently there can be some latency when executing an interact. With a good Internet connection and the forthcoming 5G national networks, the latency issue will be minimized.
